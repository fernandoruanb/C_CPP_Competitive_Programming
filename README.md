## About This Journey

I decided to enter the world of **competitive programming** with a clear purpose: to sharpen my abilities across multiple branches of computer science while continuously solving new problems, facing new constraints, and training my mind under pressure.

This is not only about writing code faster.

It is about becoming a stronger thinker.

It is about learning how to interpret complex scenarios, break them apart, identify patterns, and design efficient solutions with clarity. It is also about building the discipline required to grow through repetition, refinement, and constant exposure to hard challenges.

My objective is to train consistently with the guidance of strong mentors and use this path as preparation for:

- programming contests,
- hackathons,
- technical interviews,
- international team collaboration,
- and deeper professional maturity in software engineering.

---

## Why Codeforces?

Among the many platforms available, I chose **Codeforces** as the central pillar of this journey.

LeetCode and HackerRank are valuable platforms and have their place. However, Codeforces offers something that I consider especially powerful: it often forces the student to **understand what is happening first**, before even thinking about the solution itself.

That difference matters.

In many real situations, the hardest part is not the syntax, nor the implementation, nor even the algorithm alone. The hardest part is understanding the scenario correctly, identifying what is truly being asked, and seeing through the noise.

That is exactly why this platform resonates with me.

Much like major exams such as **ENEM**, **Fuvest**, **SAT**, and **ACT**, strong interpretation and contextual understanding are becoming increasingly essential. In many cases, reading well and thinking clearly matter even more than merely memorizing formulas.

Because of that, I see Codeforces not just as a programming platform, but as a training environment for a stronger technical mind.

## My Core Vision

I strongly believe that a junior entering the market should not grow passively.

A serious professional should be exposed to continuous and strategic training in their field.

That includes:

- algorithmic thinking,
- data structures,
- software design,
- modern development practices,
- cybersecurity awareness,
- and contact with high-level specialists.

In my view, the ideal growth path includes learning not only from programming teachers and algorithm mentors, but also from professionals in specialized areas such as cybersecurity, offensive security, and modern engineering workflows.

A junior with ambition should seek training from people who operate at a high level.

That means learning from strong programmers, experienced engineers, serious mentors, and, when relevant, specialists connected to ecosystems such as **OffSec**, **EC-Council**, and advanced security communities — always with the goal of becoming more capable, more aware, and more prepared for real-world challenges.

Through competitive programming, I aim to develop:

---

## Training Philosophy

My approach is not based on blind repetition.

It is based on **intentional training**.

That means:

1. understanding the problem deeply before rushing into code,
2. identifying patterns and reusable ideas,
3. improving solution quality over time,
4. learning from stronger people,
5. and using modern tools responsibly to accelerate growth.

Yes, that includes the intelligent use of **AI tools**.

I do not see modern AI as something to fear or ignore. I see it as one more instrument available in the modern world. The point is not to become dependent on it, but to know how to use it properly: to review ideas, compare approaches, accelerate feedback loops, refine understanding, and explore better possibilities outside official competitive settings.

Deep understanding still matters.

But in the modern era, knowing how to combine strong fundamentals with powerful tools is also part of becoming a stronger engineer.

## Competitive Programming and the Real World

To me, competitive programming is not isolated from reality.

It connects directly with many practical areas of computing:

- writing efficient code,
- handling constraints,
- improving debugging discipline,
- understanding performance,
- developing resilience,
- thinking clearly under pressure,
- and learning how to collaborate with technically demanding people.

Beyond contests, this type of training can reinforce growth in:

- software engineering,
- systems programming,
- cybersecurity,
- technical interviews,
- hackathons,
- research-oriented problem solving,
- and high-performance team environments.

That is why I see this repository not as a collection of random exercises, but as a structured part of a much larger professional formation.
